Initializing Design Thinking and Metrics into the Product Team

  • Role: Lead Designer
  • Timeframe: 2014 - 2021

Role

As the design lead of a budding product team, I introduced a series of standards, tools, and processes that became the framework for a user centric technology team.

Background

At TrueChoice, we build web apps that quantify user preferences and allow us to share quantifiable and actionable insights with our customers. The design process for each project was 2-3 weeks of drawing and pitching multiple design concepts that reinterpreted the client’s branding. The UI, the fonts, and the layout were moved around in order to add to the diversity of each concept. Development was another 1-2 weeks.

Summary

In 2014, when we started to transition the technology from from Flash to HTML5, I took the opportunity to address scalability in our delivery process by establishing a standard UI template that followed usability heuristics. This was a catalyst into more user-centric design processes, research, and user testing. A year later, we decreased the delivery process for each client from 4 weeks to a few days — and by 2020, to only a few hours. The time saved was invested into our new design values centered around identifying and solving problems for our users, and helps move TrueChoice’s products forward.

Rather than share one traditional project, each phase encapsulates a major achievement in my journey as a designer and UX leader at TrueChoice:

  1. Phase 1: Establishing Fundamentals
    1. I evangelized a base user-centric design process compiled of usability heuristics and research-backed decisions while designing the first iteration of the HTML5 app.
    2. I started usability tests with the internal team. In the early stage, this was enough to collect feedback and make discoveries for improvement.
    3. I also started documentation and training processes to pass on our learnings to future designers. These fundamentals were adopted by the design team and used to design new features and products. After the HTML5 app was released, I leveraged my experience to redesign a product recommendation page for TrueChoice's Predictive Selling app. I also guided other designers when they worked on designing new types of interactions.
  2. Phase 2: Tracking Metrics & Extending User Research
    1. I used existing metrics to push initiatives and track successes of the features we were building. I reached out to the development team to install new metrics when there were gaps. These reports were used to help gather executive buy-in on areas of improvement. I also worked with the development team to create internal tools like Bowser which visualize and communicate usage data.
    2. I reached out to our COO and CEO to set up a process and budget for usability testing outside of the company. I set up NDA forms before reaching out to “friends of the firm” for the first few rounds of testing, and then finally set up a budget for more testers who are key in ensuring regular tests. We documented our findings and held organization-wide debriefings summarizing the takeaways.
    3. During the 2020 pandemic, we started remote testing sessions. We hope to leave an archive of recordings for future designers and researchers at TrueChoice.
  3. Phase 3: The Path Forward
    1. I built a fountain of resources for the team to pull from. The senior designers are able to help train their juniors, and we understand how to work across departments to develop meaningful changes to the product — by making new mistakes.
    2. As I continue to mature as a leader, I started working with our executive stakeholders to determine key strategic goals for company. I help define and set the vision for TrueChoice’s product offerings: Predictive HR, Predictive Selling, and more.

Phase 1: Establishing Fundamentals

Practicing User-centric Design

My first iteration of the redesigned HTML5 application in 2014 applied usability heuristics that were already researched and established. I gathered research findings from Neilsen Norman Group and Baymard Institute, and best practices from web articles and books like Steve Krug's Don't Make Me Think. Apple and Google were also publishing UI guidelines that we were able to borrow from.

Since most of the user research was already done, I approached the recreation of the original Flash UI like a model kit where I was simply putting the pieces together. From the research, we knew buttons and fonts had to be a certain size. When setting the scope with the VP of Tech, we knew certain customizations and flairs like font families and animations were going to come later. Our prime focus was on the usability and so the fun part was designing the responsive UI. None of the parts fit on the popular 320px wide iPhone. (Don't worry Android fans, we also looked at and tested on non-Apple devices.)

Rapid Prototyping and Internal Usability Testing

At first, we waited until after a beta version was developed before I could test it with teammates who did not directly work on the project. Part of the reason was to maintain a grand unveiling to build excitement within the organization. While we did receive a lot of excitement and praise, we learned that this was a mistake because this first iteration took us weeks to build and did not meet all the requirements of our analytics team. From then on, we interviewed representatives from each department for key requirements. The remaining modicum of secrecy was to maintain testers. I didn't understand until later but we didn't reach out to users because we weren't allowed direct access to the participants who use our app, nor testing candidates because there were concerns of leaking sensitive IP.

These rugged usability tests were excellent for collecting feedback and discovering opportunities for improvement, however, we were still working with developers to implement and I needed to get my ideas in front of people quicker. InVision was only a few years old at this time and their product was helpful for plugging in our wireframes for quick tests and demos. I also tried POP by Marvel as an even quicker way to turn paper drawings into a prototype. Very soon, rapid prototyping became an integral part of an iterative process that allowed us to make mistakes and validate faster. These techniques carried us for a few years as we continued to recreate TrueChoice's core offerings that were still left behind in Flash.

Phase 2: Tracking Metrics & Extending User Research

Adding Metrics

Continuing on the path of research-based design, we started to leverage metrics to track basic usage like time spent in each part of the application, device population, and how often each buttons were interacted with. In 2018, we recognized that about half of our users were on a mobile device and I managed a suite of improvements to key parts of the mobile experience. In 2019, when we were improving the mobile version of Part 2, my team and I defined metrics that would help us measure the level of engagement of the feature. Being able to seeing a 50% increase in engagements (or “moved sliders”) was a huge success for the mobile experience and to our capabilities as a product team.

When the organization realized how valuable metrics were, I worked with the development team to make it easier for the team to gather. Previously, we had to log into a outdated backend portal to fetch spreadsheets. I described the key data, tables, and visuals for the development team to create an internal tool we named Bowser. Bowser periodically fetched and published data for each project in Dropbox. The spreadsheets were no more, and we could now visualize and more easily communicate usage data for each app. Bowser continues to receive feature updates each year.

Extending User Research

By 2018, we has designed and redesigned our UI a few times but we didn't have a way to get feedback. The internal testing already grew beyond its usefulness. I proposed the value of external usability tests to our COO and CEO and was met with excitement and concern. We decided that I would prepare NDA forms to ensure the company's safety; I wrote something short and friendly that we could use. For the first few rounds of testing, we decided to only reach out to “friends of the firm”. This was a low-cost approach to try our ability to facilitate tests and provide meaningful insights. The COO even provided his family members as testers. I had my own list of testers but it was nice to have the support and buy-in.

The first round of end-to-end tests in 2018 helped us gain a base understanding of how the application was received and where we might improve. The second round happened in 2019 after a set of UX enhancements. These were both held in person and we were not set up to record them. However, we diligently documented our findings and held an organization-wide debriefing summarizing the takeaways. For example, in the second round we discovered:

  • We had revamped the onboarding and instructions in the application and wanted to gauge the effectiveness. We found out that having multiple moving GIFs on a page made it distracting to read (this was a common request from our partners) and could now confidently communicate this issue while proposing an alternate solution.
  • The instructional pages we added helped users understand the application better. In the first round, all testers guessed that there were 16 or so parts in the app. In the second, most correctly answered and described all 3 parts.
  • We always knew our Part 2 UI was confusing but now we finally had clues. This insight was valuable and very exciting since the challenge was left unsolved for over 10 years. We revised and validated this in 2021.

Following these successes, I was finally able to set up a budget for sourcing testers, ensuring regular tests.

Phase 3: The Path Forward

I built a fountain of resources for the team to pull from. The senior designers are able to help train their juniors, and we understand how to work across departments to develop meaningful changes to the product — by making new mistakes.

As I continue to mature as a leader, I started working with our executive stakeholders to determine key strategic goals for company. I help define and set the vision for TrueChoice’s product offerings: Predictive HR, Predictive Selling, and more.

Results

This is an ongoing journey for TrueChoice's product team but some of our major wins include:

  1. Reduced valuable time (re)designing and (re)building parts of the app that won't move the product forward.
  2. Provided a method of identifying user challenges. We are able to discover and prioritize new areas of opportunity and user pain points based on research and metrics.
  3. Gained stakeholder trust by building the product team’s credibility and ability to measure successes. Designers were no longer seen as graphic artists and were now a part of the experts guiding the future of the products.
